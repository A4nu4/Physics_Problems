To calculate the centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) of a person at the equator, we consider the circular motion caused by the Earth's rotation around its axis.

### Given Values:

* **Radius of Earth ($R$):** $6378 \text{ km} = 6,378,000 \text{ meters}$
* **Rotation Period of Earth ($T$):** Approximately $24 \text{ hours}$
* $T = 24 \times 3600 \text{ s} = 86,400 \text{ seconds}$



---

### Step-by-Step Solution:

#### 1. Calculate the Angular Velocity ($\omega$)

Angular velocity is the rate at which the Earth rotates, measured in radians per second:


$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$

$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{86,400} \approx 7.27 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s}$$

#### 2. Calculate Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)

Centripetal acceleration for an object in circular motion is given by the formula:


$$a_c = \omega^2 \cdot R$$

Substituting the values:


$$a_c = (7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s})^2 \cdot 6,378,000 \text{ m}$$

$$a_c \approx (5.288 \times 10^{-9}) \cdot 6,378,000$$

$$a_c \approx 0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$$

---

### Final Result:

The centripetal acceleration at the equator is approximately **$0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$**.

### Contextual Comparison:

To put this in perspective, the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) is roughly $9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$. The centripetal acceleration is only about **0.34%** of $g$. This is why you don't feel "flung off" the Earth, though it does slightly reduce your effective weight at the equator compared to the poles.

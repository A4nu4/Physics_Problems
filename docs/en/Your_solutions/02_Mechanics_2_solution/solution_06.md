To solve this, we will track the energy of the tennis ball through each bounce. Since the ball is dropped from rest, its initial mechanical energy is entirely gravitational potential energy.



### Given Starting Values:
* **Initial height ($h_0$):** $2.0 \text{ m}$
* **Energy loss per bounce:** $30\%$ (which means it **retains $70\%$** or $0.7$ of its energy)
* **Acceleration due to gravity ($g$):** $9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$

---

### Step 1: Initial Mechanical Energy ($E_0$)

At the start, the energy is:


$$E_0 = m \cdot g \cdot h_0$$


---

### Step 2: Energy After the First Bounce ($E_1$)

The ball loses $30\%$ of its energy upon hitting the ground for the first time. The energy remaining ($E_1$) is:


$$E_1 = E_0 \cdot 0.70$$


Since $E = mgh$, the new height $h_1$ is directly proportional to the energy:


$$m \cdot g \cdot h_1 = (m \cdot g \cdot h_0) \cdot 0.70$$


$$h_1 = h_0 \cdot 0.70$$


$$h_1 = 2.0 \cdot 0.70 = 1.4 \text{ m}$$


---

### Step 3: Energy After the Second Bounce ($E_2$)

The ball hits the ground again and loses another $30\%$ of its *current* mechanical energy ($E_1$). The energy remaining ($E_2$) is:


$$E_2 = E_1 \cdot 0.70$$


Substituting $E_1$ from the previous step:


$$E_2 = (E_0 \cdot 0.70) \cdot 0.70$$


$$E_2 = E_0 \cdot (0.70)^2$$


---

### Step 4: Calculate the Final Height ($h_2$)

We solve for the height $h_2$ reached after the second bounce:


$$m \cdot g \cdot h_2 = (m \cdot g \cdot h_0) \cdot (0.70)^2$$


$$h_2 = h_0 \cdot 0.49$$


$$h_2 = 2.0 \cdot 0.49$$


$$h_2 = 0.98 \text{ m}$$


---

### Final Result:
The tennis ball will rise to a height of **$0.98 \text{ m}$** after the second bounce.

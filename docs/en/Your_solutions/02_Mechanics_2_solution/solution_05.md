### Given Starting Values:
* **Mass of the runner ($m_1$):** $70 \text{ kg}$
* **Initial velocity of the runner ($v_1$):** $3 \text{ m/s}$
* **Mass of the cart ($m_2$):** $140 \text{ kg}$
* **Initial velocity of the cart ($v_2$):** $0 \text{ m/s}$ (stationary)

---

### 1. Calculate the Final Speed ($V_f$)

This is a **perfectly inelastic collision** because the runner and the cart move together as a single unit after the interaction. The total momentum before the jump must equal the total momentum after.

**The Momentum Equation:**

$$m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2) V_f$$


**Substituting the known values:**

$$(70 \text{ kg} \cdot 3 \text{ m/s}) + (140 \text{ kg} \cdot 0 \text{ m/s}) = (70 \text{ kg} + 140 \text{ kg}) \cdot V_f$$


**Simplifying the terms:**

$$210 + 0 = 210 \cdot V_f$$


**Solving for $V_f$:**

$$V_f = \frac{210}{210}$$

$$V_f = 1 \text{ m/s}$$

---

### 2. Is Kinetic Energy Conserved?

To determine if kinetic energy ($KE$) is conserved, we compare the total energy before and after the collision.

**Initial Kinetic Energy ($KE_i$):**

$$KE_i = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_2^2$$

$$KE_i = \frac{1}{2} (70) (3^2) + 0$$

$$KE_i = 315 \text{ J}$$


**Final Kinetic Energy ($KE_f$):**

$$KE_f = \frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2) V_f^2$$

$$KE_f = \frac{1}{2} (210) (1^2)$$

$$KE_f = 105 \text{ J}$$

---

### Explanation

**No, kinetic energy is not conserved.** In this collision, the kinetic energy decreased from **$315 \text{ J}$** to **$105 \text{ J}$**. This loss of **$210 \text{ J}$** occurs because the collision is **inelastic**. The "missing" energy is converted into other forms, primarily **thermal energy** (heat) and **sound energy** generated at the moment the runner lands on the cart.

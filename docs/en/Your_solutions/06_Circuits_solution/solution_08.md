To find the equation for the voltage across the resistor, we apply Ohm's Law in the time domain, which states that $V(t) = I(t) \cdot R$.

### 1. Identify the Given Values

* **Current Function:** $I(t) = 2 \sin(120\pi t)$
* **Resistance:** $R = 50\,\Omega$

---

### 2. Derive the Voltage Equation

Substituting the current function and the resistance value into Ohm's Law:


$$V(t) = [2 \sin(120\pi t)] \cdot 50$$

Multiplying the peak current ($I_{max} = 2 \text{ A}$) by the resistance:


$$V(t) = (2 \times 50) \sin(120\pi t)$$

$$V(t) = 100 \sin(120\pi t)$$

---

### 3. Circuit Analysis Summary

* **Peak Voltage ($V_{max}$):** **100 V**
* **Angular Frequency ($\omega$):** $120\pi \text{ rad/s}$
* **Frequency ($f$):** Since $\omega = 2\pi f$, the frequency is **60 Hz**.
* **Phase Relationship:** In a purely resistive circuit, the voltage and current are **in phase**, meaning they both reach their maximum and minimum values at the same time.

The final equation for the voltage is **$V(t) = 100 \sin(120\pi t)$**.

To find the average current of the lightning bolt, we first utilize the formula relating charge, current, and time.

### 1. Identify the Formula for Charge

The total charge transferred is defined as the product of the average current and the time interval:


$$Q = I_{avg} \cdot t$$

---

### 2. Identify the Given Values

* **Charge ($Q$):** $30 \text{ C}$
* **Time ($t$):** $2 \text{ ms} = 0.002 \text{ s}$

---

### 3. Calculate the Average Current ($I_{avg}$)

Rearranging the charge formula to solve for current:


$$I_{avg} = \frac{Q}{t}$$

$$I_{avg} = \frac{30 \text{ C}}{0.002 \text{ s}}$$

$$I_{avg} = 15,000 \text{ A}$$

The average current of the lightning bolt is **$15,000 \text{ A}$** (or $15 \text{ kA}$).

For perspective, a typical household circuit breaker is usually rated between **15 A** and **20 A**, meaning this lightning bolt carries roughly **750 to 1,000 times** the current capacity of a standard home circuit.

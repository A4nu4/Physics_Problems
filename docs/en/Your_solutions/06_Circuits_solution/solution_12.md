To determine the voltages and currents in a transformer, we use the relationships between the number of turns, voltage, and current based on the principle of electromagnetic induction.

### 1. Secondary Voltage ($V_s$)

The relationship between the number of turns and the voltage in a transformer is given by the transformer turns ratio:


$$\frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$$

**Given Values:**

* **Primary Turns ($N_p$):** 1000
* **Secondary Turns ($N_s$):** 200
* **Primary Voltage ($V_p$):** $120\text{ V}$

**Calculation:**


$$V_s = V_p \cdot \left( \frac{N_s}{N_p} \right)$$

$$V_s = 120\text{ V} \cdot \left( \frac{200}{1000} \right)$$

$$V_s = 120\text{ V} \cdot 0.2 = \mathbf{24\text{ V}}$$

This is a **step-down transformer** because the secondary voltage is lower than the primary voltage.

---

### 2. Primary Current ($I_p$)

In an ideal transformer, the power in the primary coil equals the power in the secondary coil ($P_p = P_s$). This creates an inverse relationship between voltage and current:


$$\frac{I_p}{I_s} = \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$$

**Given Values:**

* **Secondary Current ($I_s$):** $3\text{ A}$

**Calculation:**


$$I_p = I_s \cdot \left( \frac{N_s}{N_p} \right)$$

$$I_p = 3\text{ A} \cdot \left( \frac{200}{1000} \right)$$

$$I_p = 3\text{ A} \cdot 0.2 = \mathbf{0.6\text{ A}}$$

---

### Summary of Transformer Results

| Parameter | Primary ($p$) | Secondary ($s$) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Number of Turns ($N$)** | 1000 | 200 |
| **Voltage ($V$)** | $120\text{ V}$ | **$24\text{ V}$** |
| **Current ($I$)** | **$0.6\text{ A}$** | $3\text{ A}$ |

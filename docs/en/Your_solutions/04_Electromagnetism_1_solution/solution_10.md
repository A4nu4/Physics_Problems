To calculate the magnetic force on a current-carrying wire, we use the formula derived from the Lorentz force. When a wire is placed in a magnetic field, the moving charges within the wire experience a collective force.



### The Force Formula
The magnitude of the magnetic force ($F_B$) on a straight wire is given by:

$$F_B = I \cdot L \cdot B \cdot \sin(\theta)$$

Where:
* **$I$ (Current):** $10 \text{ A}$
* **$L$ (Length):** $2.0 \text{ m}$
* **$B$ (Magnetic Field):** $0.5 \text{ T}$
* **$\theta$:** The angle between the direction of the current and the magnetic field.

---

### a) Angle is $90^\circ$ (Perpendicular)
When the wire is perpendicular to the field, the force is at its maximum because $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$.

$$F_B = (10 \text{ A}) \cdot (2.0 \text{ m}) \cdot (0.5 \text{ T}) \cdot \sin(90^\circ)$$
$$F_B = 10 \cdot 2.0 \cdot 0.5 \cdot 1$$
$$F_B = 10 \text{ N}$$



---

### b) Angle is $45^\circ$
At this angle, only the component of the magnetic field perpendicular to the wire contributes to the force. Since $\sin(45^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707$:

$$F_B = (10 \text{ A}) \cdot (2.0 \text{ m}) \cdot (0.5 \text{ T}) \cdot \sin(45^\circ)$$
$$F_B = 10 \cdot 0.707$$
$$F_B \approx 7.07 \text{ N}$$

---

### c) Angle is $0^\circ$ (Parallel)
When the wire is parallel to the magnetic field, the current is moving in the same direction as the field lines. Since $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$:

$$F_B = (10 \text{ A}) \cdot (2.0 \text{ m}) \cdot (0.5 \text{ T}) \cdot \sin(0^\circ)$$
$$F_B = 0 \text{ N}$$



---

### Final Results Summary:
| Angle ($\theta$) | Magnetic Force ($F_B$) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **$90^\circ$** | $10 \text{ N}$ |
| **$45^\circ$** | $7.07 \text{ N}$ |
| **$0^\circ$** | $0 \text{ N}$ |

The direction of these forces (for the $90^\circ$ and $45^\circ$ cases) would be determined by the **Right-Hand Rule**, pointing perpendicular to both the wire and the magnetic field lines.

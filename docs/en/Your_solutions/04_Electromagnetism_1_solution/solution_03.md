To find the equilibrium position for the third charge, we locate the point where the net electric force acting on it is zero. This occurs when the repulsive force from $q_1$ exactly balances the repulsive force from $q_2$.



### Given Starting Values:
* **Charge 1 ($q_1$):** $+4 \text{ C}$
* **Charge 2 ($q_2$):** $+9 \text{ C}$
* **Charge 3 ($q_3$):** $+1 \text{ C}$
* **Total Separation ($L$):** $2 \text{ m}$

---

### 1. Set up the Equilibrium Condition

Let $x$ be the distance from $q_1$ to $q_3$. Therefore, the distance from $q_3$ to $q_2$ is $(2 - x)$. For equilibrium, the magnitudes of the forces must be equal:


$$F_{13} = F_{23}$$


Using Coulomb's Law:


$$\frac{k q_1 q_3}{x^2} = \frac{k q_2 q_3}{(2 - x)^2}$$


---

### 2. Simplify the Equation

Notice that $k$ and $q_3$ appear on both sides and cancel out. This shows that the equilibrium position is independent of the magnitude or sign of the third charge:


$$\frac{q_1}{x^2} = \frac{q_2}{(2 - x)^2}$$


Substitute the values for $q_1$ and $q_2$:


$$\frac{4}{x^2} = \frac{9}{(2 - x)^2}$$


---

### 3. Solve for $x$

To solve this efficiently, take the square root of both sides:


$$\sqrt{\frac{4}{x^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{9}{(2 - x)^2}}$$


$$\frac{2}{x} = \frac{3}{2 - x}$$


Now, cross-multiply:


$$2(2 - x) = 3x$$


$$4 - 2x = 3x$$


$$4 = 5x$$


$$x = 0.8 \text{ m}$$


---

### Final Result:
The equilibrium position for $q_3$ is **$0.8 \text{ m}$** away from the $+4 \text{ C}$ charge (which is $1.2 \text{ m}$ away from the $+9 \text{ C}$ charge).

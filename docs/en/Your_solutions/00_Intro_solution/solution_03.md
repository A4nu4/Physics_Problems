To determine how the gravitational force changes, we analyze the relationship between the variables in the Universal Law of Gravitation.

### Task Conditions and Values

* **Original Force Equation:** $F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$
* **Changes Applied:**
* Distance is doubled: $r_{new} = 2r$
* Mass 1 is halved: $m_{1,new} = \frac{1}{2}m_1$
* Mass 2 is halved: $m_{2,new} = \frac{1}{2}m_2$


* **Goal:** Find the factor by which the new force $F_{new}$ differs from the original force $F$.

---

### Step-by-Step Solution

**Step 1: Write the expression for the new force**
Substitute the updated values into the gravitational formula:


$$F_{new} = G \frac{(\frac{1}{2}m_1) (\frac{1}{2}m_2)}{(2r)^2}$$

**Step 2: Simplify the numerator**
Multiply the fractions in the numerator:


$$\left( \frac{1}{2}m_1 \right) \left( \frac{1}{2}m_2 \right) = \frac{1}{4} m_1 m_2$$

**Step 3: Simplify the denominator**
Square the new distance:


$$(2r)^2 = 4r^2$$

**Step 4: Combine the changes**
Plug these simplified parts back into the equation for $F_{new}$:


$$F_{new} = G \frac{\frac{1}{4} m_1 m_2}{4r^2}$$

**Step 5: Factor out the numerical constant**
Bring the numerical coefficients to the front:


$$F_{new} = \left( \frac{1/4}{4} \right) \cdot G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

$$F_{new} = \frac{1}{16} \cdot G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

**Step 6: Compare with the original force**
Since $F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$, we can see that:


$$F_{new} = \frac{1}{16} F$$

---

### Final Answer

The gravitational force $F$ decreases by a factor of **$16$** (or changes by a factor of **$\frac{1}{16}$**).
